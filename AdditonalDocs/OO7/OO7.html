Organizational Overview 7

Structural Empowerment

 

An action plan that includes a target and demonstrates evidence of progress toward 80% of registered nurses obtaining a baccalaureate or graduate degree in nursing by 2020. Include an assessment of the current status; an evaluation of methods and strategies to increase the educational level of the workforce; and an appraisal of established, realistic targets to meet the organization's strategy to increase the number of registered nurses with a degree in nursing (baccalaureate or graduate degree).

 

 

An evaluation of the methods and strategies to increase the educational level of the workforce has been included in the below “Evaluation of strategies” table.  Please see below revised submission for OO7.

 

An action plan that includes a target and demonstrates evidence of progress toward 80% of registered nurses obtaining a baccalaureate or graduate degree in nursing by 2020. Include an assessment of the current status; an evaluation of methods and strategies to increase the educational level of the workforce; and an appraisal of established, realistic targets to meet the organization's strategy to increase the number of registered nurses with a degree in nursing (baccalaureate or graduate degree).

 

Strategies
In fiscal year 2014 (October 2013-September 2014), the Nursing Executive Leadership Team, comprising the CNO and vice presidents of nursing, evaluated and implemented six specific strategies to increase the percentage of nurses with a BSN or higher degree.  Strategies included:

  • Nurse education tracking
    • Establish sound repository for accurate collection and reporting of nursing education data through implementation of an electronic Nursing Professional Portfolio.
  • BSN Projections
    • Detailed analysis of the additional number of BSN nurses needed to comply with Institute of Medicine 80% recommendations.
    • Include goals within the Nursing Strategic Plan to support projections.
  • Assess existing resources for nurse education, and secure additional funds
  • Everyone’s A Recruiter Now (E.A.R.N.): Advertise and educate employees on the E.A.R.N. referral bonus program. (Evidence OO7-1, E.A.R.N. Internal Employee Program Flyer, Let’s Hire 100 Nurses in 100 Days! How Can I Help Recruit?)
  • Partnered with human resources to ensure tuition assistance program is comparable to other Texas Medical Center hospitals. (Evidence OO5-6, Tuition Assistance Program Policy)
  • Partnered with Development to secure donors to fund nursing scholarships. TCH nurses with associate degrees now eligible for additional funding for BSN education, up to $5250.  (Evidence OO7-3, Nursing Congress Email – Going Back To School, Voice Of Nursing Blog Post)
  • Facilitate staff access to education for BSN or higher
    • Ensure awareness and understanding of existing resources available.
    • Establish online and local educational partners.
    • Offering tuition discounts to TCH employees and on-site recruitment initiatives. (Evidence OO7-4, http://www.edassist.com/client-company/texaschildrenshospital/matrix)
  • Establish education support for incumbents and changes with new hire requirements
    • Promote flexible scheduling options.
  • Shift in hiring standards to target BSN nurses.
  • CNO approval is required for all associate degree nurses (ADN) before hiring.
  • The nurse is required to sign a commitment to enroll in a BSN program within six months of hire date, with a plan to graduate within two years of entering that program. (Evidence OO7-5, Commitment to Enrolling to a BSN Program)
  • Hardwire processes for monitoring BSN rate progress
    • Quarterly review of the BSN rate
    • Quarterly assessment of the nursing strategic plan

 

Evaluation of Strategies

 

Strategies

Evaluation

Nursing Education Tracking

Nurses input their education, certification, and other work accomplishments in an electronic Nursing Professional Portfolio.  In October 2016, there was a change of vendor from Decision Critical/Nursing Portfolio to HealthStream. In reviewing the data, we are currently working with the vendor to improve the collection and reporting of data for our organization.  This Nursing Education Tracking tool has not directly impacted the BSN rate, but can provide a solid foundation for measurement of progress.

BSN Projections

In May 2014, a forecasting tool was utilized to determine the predicted percentage of BSN nurses based on turnover and hiring statistics. This tool helped to determine the % of BSN hires needed to meet the 80% national goal.

Assess existing resources for nurse education, and secure additional funds

E.A.R.N. – When initiating the hiring of 100 RNs in 100 days campaign, the nurse leaders and nurse managers evaluated the E.A.R.N. program and opportunities to increase its awareness to employees. Certain RN positions were placed in “Super Red Hot” or “Red Hot” job categories, which would reward the employee up to $5000. Nurses hired from the E.A.R.N. program include 51 nurses in 2014, 86 nurses in 2015, and 116 nurses in 2016, demonstrating an increase over time.

Currently, TCH tuition reimbursement is $2500 annually for full-time employees and $1500 annually for part-time employees.  Texas Children’s Hospital tuition assistance program is reviewed annually and continues to be competitive and aligned with other hospitals in the surrounding area.  

The additional BSN scholarship assistance has been identified as the best strategy to increase the organization’s BSN rate. In December 2013, a survey was conducted of ADN/Diploma Registered Nurses, where 100 of 194 respondents stated that finances was the number one barrier to returning back to school. On June 10, 2015, Texas Children’s received an Exxon/Mobile grant to provide supplemental tuition assistance to ADN/Diploma Registered Nurses enrolled or enrolling in a RN-BSN program, in addition to the current TCH tuition reimbursement amounts listed above.  Full-time nurses are eligible for an additional $2750 per semester and part-time nurses are eligible for an additional $1375 per semester under this grant.  As of November 2016, 111 (92 full-time, 19 part-time) Texas Children’s ADN/Diploma nurses have enrolled in a RN-BSN program benefiting from the supplemental tuition assistance. In addition, 39 nurses have graduated with the assistance of supplemental funding. The total amount of funding paid out from June 2015 to November 2016 has exceeded $214,000 with more than $15,000 in additional funds pending. This program has a positive impact on nurses obtaining their BSN and effectively shown an improvement in the BSN rate.

Facilitate Staff Access to Education for BSN or higher

The majority of clinical nurses’ in the inpatient and Ambulatory setting educational needs are met through the annual TCH Nursing Professional Day Conference. The annual 2016 Nursing Professional Day Conference was held in Summer 2016 at the Medical Center Campus, and in Fall 2016 at the West Campus in Katy. TCH educational partners, including sixteen local and online universities, were present during this conference to answer questions nurses had in regards to their RN-BSN, MSN or DNP programs.

Texas Children’s Tuition Assistance program provides both education advising and tuition prepay/reimbursement.  TCH partners with EdAssist (an education management company) that networks with a variety of schools to offer tuition discount for TCH employees.  Both the education advising and tuition prepay/reimbursement have been effective ways to support nurses to achieve a BSN or higher degrees. 

Establish Education Support for Incumbents and Changes With New Hire Requirements

Nurse leaders and nurse managers encourage staff nurses to return to school and work individually with their employees to balance work and school schedules to ensure nurses are successful in reaching their educational goals.

 

Nurse Leaders evaluate the percentage of ADN/Diploma and BSN nurses hired annually to ensure an increase of BSN hires over time.  As noted below, the percentage of hiring BSN or higher degree nurses has continued to increase over the past four years:

  • 2013 – 18% ADNs/Diploma Nurses hired

                  82% BSN or higher level of degree  hired

  • 2014 – 16% ADNs/Diploma Nurses hired

                  84% BSN or higher level of degree  hired

  • 2015 – 13% ADNs/Diploma Nurses hired

                  87% BSN or higher level of degree  hired

  • 2016 – 13% ADNs/Diploma Nurses hired

                  87% BSN or higher level of degree  Hired

 

Effective November 2013, all ADN/Diploma Nurses required an approval for hire from the Chief Nurse Officer. After approval, the human resource department obtained a verbal agreement with the ADN/Diploma Nurse to enroll in a RN-BSN program within six months. In 2015, nurse leaders and nurse managers evaluated this process and noted some ADN/Diploma Nurses were non-compliant with the verbal agreement. In January 2016, the new Chief Nurse Officer implemented a written contract to the process. This written contract requires the nurse to enroll in a BSN program within six months of hire and a plan to graduate within two years of entering the program. There is a centralized tracking mechanism for all ADN/diploma nurses to ensure staff are meeting their educational milestones.  As of November 2016, 24 nurses have received their BSN, and 52 nurses are currently enrolled in a RN-BSN program which has successfully met the expectation of the ADN/Diploma contract, and directly influenced the BSN rate.

Hardwire Processes for Monitoring BSN Rate Progress

On a monthly basis, Sarah Marcion, BSN, RN, Assistant Director of Magnet, reports data on the percentage of nurses with a BSN and higher degree to Emily Weber, MS, RN, NEA-BC, Director of Acute Care Services & Magnet Program Director and Mary Jo André, MSN, RN, NEA-BC, Senior Vice President, Chief Nursing Officer.  In addition, every other month, Marcion presents the percentage report to Nursing Coordinating Council (NCC), one of the shared governance councils.  By presenting the BSN report to the NCC, this promotes awareness of the strategic plan goal to increase the BSN rate of 80% or higher.  This has brought awareness and influences the BSN hiring practices at the unit level.

The Nursing Strategic Plan is assessed and updated annually.

 

Assessment of Current Status
In June 2016, the Nursing Executive Council (NEC) assessed the current percentage of direct care nurses practicing at Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH) with a BSN or higher degree. The NEC comprises the senior vice president/chief nursing officer (CNO), executive vice president, executive vice president (Nursing and Pavilion for Women), executives – those with nursing direct reports – including CNO executive reports, Ambulatory, Surgical Services, Pavilion for Women, West Campus/
Woodlands/The Centers for Women and Children, Texas Children’s Pediatrics, executive of Human Resources, executive Ancillary Services and Research, executive of Care Management and Family Services, and executive of Quality and Safety. Their assessment demonstrated that 80.37% of nurses have a BSN or higher degree, and that this percentage has increased each year since 2013.

 

 

Evidence OO7-1, Percentage of Nurses with a BSN or Higher Degree

 

On a quarterly basis, Sarah Marcion, BSN, RN, Assistant Director of Magnet, reports data on the percentage of nurses with a BSN or higher degree to Emily Weber, MS, RN, NEA-BC, Director of Acute Care Services & Magnet Program Director, and Mary Jo André, MSN, RN, NEA-BC, Senior Vice President, Chief Nursing Officer. In addition, Marcion presents the percentage report to the Nursing Coordinating Council (NCC) every other month. NCC comprises nursing leadership from across all campuses and shared governance officers from each council. This keeps all nurse leaders informed in order to maintain the current performance of 80% nurses with a BSN or higher degree. (Evidence OO7-6, Nursing Coordinating Council July 2016 Attendance Sheet, Meeting Minutes and Presentation)

 

Establishing Future Goals
The Nursing Executive Council determined that the strategies set in place in fiscal year 2014 have been effective in increasing TCH’s percentage of nurses with a BSN or higher degree, which was above the NDNQI benchmarking group. To maintain current performance and continue to increase the percentage of nurses with a BSN or higher degree, the NEC set a goal for FY 2016 through 2018 to increase the BSN degree nurse rate to over 80%. The strategic plan is readily available for all employees to view on the TCH intranet, Connect website, under “Nursing Resources.” (Evidence OO7-7, Texas Children’s Hospital Intranet Connect Page, Nursing Resources, Nursing Strategic Plan)
