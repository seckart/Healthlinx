Example One: Neonatal Cooling – Care Coordination and Continuity through Interprofessional Collaborative Practice


Background
Hypoxemic ischemic encephalopathy (HIE), caused by perinatal asphyxia, is a significant cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality. Therapeutic hypothermia has emerged as a state of the art treatment for neonates with HIE. The challenge has been to ensure that the cooling of the infant is initiated upon arrival to the outlying hospital and is continued as care is transitioned to the Newborn Center team at Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH).

 

In November 2013, TCH Transport Services, in conjunction with the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse experts, physicians and respiratory therapists, reviewed evidence-based literature on the cooling of patients with HIE which showed that neurodevelopmental outcomes are improved for 1-3 per 1,000 live births if the intervention of active cooling is initiated within six hours of birth. Based on this identified need and evidence-based practice literature, the Therapeutic Hypothermia Work Group developed a TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol specifically for transporting and cooling neonate patients with HIE. The Therapeutic Hypothermia Work Group selected the CritiCool cooling device to be used with care coordination and continuity of care during transport and continued through admission. Using the same cooling device during transport and the hospital stay ensures a consistent cooling methodology. (Evidence EP5a-a, Guidelines for Acute Care of the Neonate, Transport Cooling Process, Cooling Admission Checklist)

 

In January 2014, Jennifer Bee, BSN, RN, CCRN, Decentralized Education Coordinator, Transport Services, and Allison Walton, BSN, RNC-NIC, Decentralized Education Coordinator, Newborn Center NICU, created the Total Body Cooling PowerPoint presentation. This was incorporated into the education on the use of the TCH Neonatal Cooling Protocol, which was provided beginning in February 2014. Bee and Walton developed education in the form of in-services for the Transport Services clinical experts and the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurses. To ensure the success of this initiative, consistent education would need to be provided to the transport team and the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurses, physicians and respiratory therapists. A PowerPoint was used for the education of all groups, and a monthly newsletter that included updates was distributed through email in the Newborn Center NICU and posted in designated areas. (Evidence EP5-2, Total Body Cooling Education PowerPoint)  The clinical impact of coordinated and continuous care for this critical patient population was recognized nationally. Bee and Tonya Jack, MSN, RN, CCRN, CMTE, Manager Clinical Transport, Transport Services, were accepted to present “Neonatal Cooling on Ground Transports” at an American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) Conference section on Transport Medicine. (Evidence EP5-4, Cooling Poster)

Nurses’ Involvement in Interprofessional Collaborative Practice for Care Coordination and Continuity of Care
When the TCH Transfer Center (currently known as the Nerve Center) is contacted by the referring hospital with a transfer request for an HIE patient who requires total body cooling, the call is received by a transport communication specialist to start the intake process. The transport communication specialist pages the transport team, and the call is then transferred to the Transport Services charge nurse for the continuation of the intake process. The Transport Services charge nurse conferences in the Newborn Center Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU) transport attending physician to complete the intake and for coordination of care. The NICU transport attending physician receives report from the referring physician to discuss whether the patient meets the inclusion criteria for the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol. Once the NICU transport attending physician accepts the transfer of the patient, the Transport Services charge nurse notifies the transport communication specialist in the Transfer Center of the acceptance for transfer. The Transport Services charge nurse continues care coordination with the referring physician or registered nurse by requesting a fax number to submit the TCH Neonatal Cooling Protocol. The instructions to initiate cooling include:

  • Check axillary temperature every 15 minutes
  • Stop the cooling process and turn the warmer back on if the patient’s temperature falls below 33.5°C
  • Turn the warmer off if patient’s temperature is above 34.5°C
  • Contact the Transport Services charge nurse with questions or recommendations related to the cooling patient

 

The transport communication specialist informs the referring physician or designee of the name of the accepting physician. The Transport Services charge nurse notifies the Newborn Center NICU charge nurse of the patient’s medical history.

 

The Transport Services team receives a request for the transport of a patient with a diagnosis of HIE and the Transport Services nurse begins the process by obtaining the CritiCool cooling device for transport.

The Transport Services team, which consists of a clinical nurse, respiratory therapist, transport emergency medical technician (EMT) and neonatal nurse practitioner (if patient condition is unstable), travels to receive the patient from the referring hospital. On arrival to the referring hospital unit, the Transport Services nurse, the neonatal nurse practitioner and the respiratory therapist receive updated patient report, assess the patient and formulate a plan of care for transport. The Transport Services nurse or neonatal nurse practitioner will contact the Newborn Center NICU transport attending physician to discuss the patient findings and transport care plan. In preparation for transfer, the Transport Service team completes the following:

  • Transport nurse and respiratory therapist place patient in CritiCool blanket
  • Transport nurse places rectal temperature probe
  • Transport nurse and respiratory therapist record temperature every 15 minutes
  • Patient secured in neutral thermal environment by Transport Services team
  • Transport nurse and neonatal nurse practitioner obtain transport consent, and family is updated on plan of care

 

The Transport Services team loads the patient in the ambulance, and the transport nurse initiates the active cooling. The transport nurse or neonatal nurse practitioner calls the Newborn Center NICU charge nurse 30 minutes prior to arrival to give an updated report for continuity of care. During the transfer, the transport nurse and respiratory therapist monitor and document the patient’s vital signs, airway and neurological status.

 

Upon arrival to TCH, the patient is transported to the Newborn Center NICU, where the Transport Services team transitions the care of the patient over to the Newborn Center NICU interprofessional team, which comprises clinical nurses, a neonatal nurse practitioner, a critical care physician and a respiratory therapist. The Transport Services team gives report on the patient to the parents and the interprofessional team for continuation of the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol. In the initial assessment, the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse:

  • Unwraps the patient
  • Removes the rectal temperature probe
  • Inserts the esophageal temperature probe
  • Re-wraps the patient in the cooling blanket
  • Starts the CritiCool machine

 

The Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse assists the respiratory therapist with securement of the patient’s airway. The neonatal nurse practitioner, in collaboration with the critical care physician, places the orders to continue the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol. The neonatal nurse practitioner reviews the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol and the plan of care with the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse. If intravenous access is needed, the neonatal nurse practitioner or critical care physician attempts to place an umbilical arterial catheter or an umbilical venous catheter. The Newborn Center clinical nurse assists the electroencephalogram (EEG) technician with placement of the electrodes for continuous video EEG monitoring. During the 72 hours of cooling, the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse closely monitors the patient’s vital signs and neurological status until the patient achieves an esophageal core temperature of 33.5°C.

 

During the patient’s admission:

  • The Newborn Center NICU charge nurse leads the interprofessional collaborative team in daily care progression rounds to discuss:
    •  the patient’s history
    •  the physical exam assessment
    •  the plan of care to identify any potential needs or barriers

     

  • The Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse leads beside rounds with the parent and the interprofessional team twice a day to discuss:
    •  the active problems
    •  the reason the patient is in Newborn Center NICU
    •  concerns from the past 24 hours and the plan of care

 

The members of the interprofessional team document in the daily progress notes. After 72 hours of cooling, the rewarming process begins, with the goal temperature of 36.5°C.  Once the goal temperature is achieved, the neonatal nurse practitioner schedules a neonatal head magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with spectroscopy. If the patient’s neurological status and airway is stable, the neonatal nurse practitioner discontinues the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol, including the ventilator and the EEG monitoring. (Evidence EP5a-b, Transfer Center Cooling Process, Neonatal Cooling Protocol, NICU-Total Body Cooling Process, NICU Bedside Rounds, Language Barrier Procedure)

 

Patient Example of Care Coordination and Continuity of Care through Interprofessional Collaborative Practice
In August 2016, the TCH Transfer Center received a call from a referring hospital requesting transfer of a delivered 39-week infant diagnosed with HIE for total body cooling. Due to antepartum and delivery complications, the infant experienced severe respiratory distress with neurological deficits, which required a neonatal nurse practitioner for transport. The transport communication specialist initiated the intake process, paged the transport team and transferred the call to the Transport Services charge nurse for the continuation of the intake process. The Transport Services team, comprising the transport nurse, neonatal nurse practitioner, respiratory therapist and an EMT, prepared for the transport. The transport nurse retrieved the CritiCool device for transport. The Transport Services charge nurse conferenced the Newborn Center NICU transport attending physician for report and acceptance of the transfer. Once the Newborn Center NICU transport attending physician determined the patient met the inclusion criteria for the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol, the Transport Services charge nurse completed the intake and began the coordination of care by asking the nurse at the referring hospital to initiate the TCH Neonatal Cooling Protocol. The Transport Services charge nurse notified the transport communication specialist of acceptance for transfer and notified the Newborn Center NICU charge of the patient’s medical history.

 

On arrival to the referring hospital, the Transport Services team received an update on the patient’s status from the referring hospital unit. The Transport Services team, Elizabeth Mallet, BSN, RN, CCRN, Christopher Dischler, MSN, APRN, NNP-BC, and Ray Estlinbaum, RRT, NPS, CNPT, assessed the patient and formulated a plan for transfer. Dischler contacted Maria Reyes, MD, the attending physician covering TCH Transport Services, to give report on the initial assessment and review the plan of care. Prior to departure, the Transport Services team took the patient to the mother’s room, where Mallet and Dischler obtained transport consent and updated the family on the plan of care using an interpreter. At five hours of age, Mallet placed the patient in the CritiCool cooling blanket, and the Transport Services team placed the patient into the transport incubator with neutral thermal environment. Mallet, Dischler and Estlinbaum secured and loaded the patient onto the ambulance and continued the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol. Mallet and Dischler monitored the patient’s vital signs and neurological status. Mallet obtained labs, started the morphine drip and administered antibiotics. Estlinbaum monitored the patient’s airway and modified the ventilator settings. Dischler, in collaboration with Mallet, called Newborn Center NICU charge nurse Leanna Copenhaver, RN to provide an updated report on the patient’s status. (Evidence EP5a-c, Memorandum of Transfer, Transport Services-Kangaroo Crew Transport Notes)

 

The patient was intubated, placed on the cooling blanket and transported to the Newborn Center NICU accompanied by the Transport Services team. Mallet, Dischler and Estlinbaum provided a full bedside report to the Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse Sheryl Wallis, RN; Copenhaver; Sasha Galan, RT, Respiratory Care Services; and Advanced Practice Provider Crystal Hockaday, MSN, APRN, NNP-BC. In collaboration with Gautham Suresh, MD, Neonatologist, attending physician at the time, Shino Thomas, MSN, APRN, NNP-BC, placed the order for the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol in the electronic medical record. Thomas reviewed the orders of the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol with Wallis to ensure the transition and continuation of care was maintained. Wallis assessed the patient and connected the patient to the CritiCool device at the bedside, while Copenhaver retrieved initial labs and contacted radiology for a chest x-ray to verify placement of the umbilical arterial catheter and the endotracheal tube. Wallis monitored the temperature continuously via esophageal probe that was confirmed by x-ray. Galan completed an assessment on the patient’s respiratory status to maintain the airway and ensure proper ventilator settings. Suresh consulted the pediatric neurologist for the initiation of continuous EEG monitoring and, with Wallis’ assistance, Anita Thompson, EEG Technician, performed the baseline EEG. Suresh completed his assessment and documented the plan of care for the interprofessional collaborative team. For additional intravenous access, Hockaday placed an umbilical venous catheter with support from Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse Jessica Gomez, RN and the supervision of Suresh, for prolonged IV therapy, medication administration and parenteral nutrition. Copenhaver contacted radiology for another chest x-ray to verify the umbilical venous catheter. (Evidence EP5a-d, Documentation in Patient Electronic Medical Record)

 

During the patient’s admission, the Newborn Center NICU interprofessional collaborative team met daily for care progression rounds led by the Newborn Center NICU charge nurse to discuss the patient’s medical history, physical assessment and the plan of care to identify any potential needs or barriers in caring for the patient. The Newborn Center NICU clinical nurse led bedside rounds twice a day with the interprofessional team to discuss the active problems, the reason the patient was in the Newborn Center NICU, concerns from the past 24 hours and the plan of care. After rounding, the Newborn Center NICU interprofessional team documented daily in the patient’s progress notes. Jacqueline Williams, RN, Care Manager, assessed the discharge planning and continued to follow the patient with the interprofessional team to assess for needs. Rose Toruno, Occupational Therapist, reviewed the patient’s chart and deferred the evaluation until the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol was completed to assess the occupational needs and readiness. 

 

On August 9, 2016, Advanced Practice Provider Whitney Sansotta, MSN, APRN, NNP-BC, noted that the patient was stable. Sansotta ordered an MRI of the brain without contrast with spectroscopy and discontinued the continuous EEG monitoring per the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol. Using an interpreter from the language services line, Sansotta provided an update to the mother that the patient was stable and the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol would be discontinued. In addition, Sansotta gained consent from the mother to use donor breast milk until she was able to provide expressed breast milk.  With the interprofessional team at the patient’s bedside, Sansotta extubated the patient, who tolerated the procedure well. Lactation assistant Sonia Castillo was notified when the mother arrived at the bedside to provide a consultation, tour of the milk bank, supplies and the breastfeeding education handout. The interprofessional collaborative team provided discharge education and made follow-up appointments with the pediatrician, neurology as well as the developmental consult. (Evidence EP5a-e, Care Progression Rounds, Patient Electronic Medical Record Notes)

 

Outcome
Nurses’ involvement in interprofessional collaborative practice and care coordination ensured the continuity of care of this patient. The patient was discharged at day 12 of life with no documented neurological deficits at the time of discharge. Texas Children’s Hospital is one of the few hospitals in Texas that provides active cooling. Since the implementation of the TCH Total Body Cooling Protocol, TCH transports each year approximately 16 HIE patients who require active cooling. TCH Transport Services and Newborn Center NICU nurses, in collaboration with the interprofessional team, provide care coordination and continuity of care for optimal neurological outcomes for this patient population.
