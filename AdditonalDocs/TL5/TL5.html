Example One:  Infusion Pump Tubing – Unplanned Change

 

Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH) has been using Alaris IV infusion pumps for large volume infusions for over 7 years. Until July of 2016, Medfusion syringe pumps were used in addition to the Alaris pumps in order to administer medications that were supplied by the pharmacy department in syringes.  Syringe drug delivery allows for more accurate administration, especially important in the management of very small neonates and critically ill children.

 

The decision was made to convert entirely to the Alaris system including the Alaris syringe pump. The need to eliminate the Medfusion syringe pump included:

  • Access to a larger drug library
  • Capability of initiating drug library updates wirelessly through CareFusion
  • Standardization of tubing with single vendor support
  • Ability to conduct closed-loop medication administration with CareFusion pumps and EPIC prospectively

 

TCH chose to adopt the Alaris syringe pump module and its associated disposables including the syringe pump tubing with a pressure sensing disc and closed medication system (Evidence TL5a-1, CareFusion PCA and Syringe Project Steering Team Meeting Notes).  The syringe pump tubing with a pressure-sensing disc enables the pump to begin the infusion quicker, and it allows the nurse to detect and act on occlusions more readily when compared to other syringe pumps in the market.  The closed medication system decreases the amount of access to the IV line, thereby decreasing the likelihood of contamination.

 

The syringe pump module and tubing was officially implemented in the Main Campus and West Campus on July 26, 2016.  The tubing with the pressure sensing disc was to be used for continuous infusions of critical medications like vasopressors, prostaglandins, inotropes, sedatives, analgesics, and insulin.  Patient care areas with a high volume of intermittent medications were to use the closed medication system.

 

A few weeks after go-live, TCH clinical nurses and nurse leaders identified an increase in reports of leaking and defective syringe pump tubing.  On August 15, 2016, nurse leader, Tarra Kerr, MSN, RN, NEA-BC, Director of Nursing, Cardiac Critical Care, convened a workgroup of nurse leaders, clinical nurses, physicians, and representatives from the Patient Safety and Quality Departments, Supply Chain Management, Clinical Informatics, and CareFusion to evaluate the reported issue and identify potential solutions to these unplanned chain of events.

TL5a, CareFusion Product Issues Meeting Workgroup

 

Kerr facilitated the collaboration between the CareFusion Product Issues Meeting Workgroup members to develop the following plan to respond immediately to the concerns: (Evidence TL5a-2, CareFusion Product Issues Meeting Workgroup and Safety Scoop Events):

  • Collect all defective product for CareFusion
  • Obtain investigative reports from CareFusion for all defective product
  • Identify affected lot numbers currently used at TCH, and investigate potential replacement products
  • Develop a communication plan to nursing regarding issue and solutions
  • Establish a daily status update
  • Clinical specialists to conduct clinical inquiries using ListServ to evaluate if other children’s hospitals were experiencing issues with the CareFusion product

 

During this critical time of unplanned change, leadership was provided by Kerr as events unfolded on almost a daily basis.

 

On August 17, 2016, Kerr guided the nurses through the unplanned change by communicating the first Safety Alert and Resolution Plan to the Nursing Director teams for distribution to their clinical staff (Evidence TL5a-3, Safety Alert Communication Email).  The action plan involved the replacement of the entire stock of pressure sensing disc tubing with specific lot numbers across the health system. These lot numbers were suspected to be defective. Tubing infusing critical drips that could not be replaced without compromising patient safety would be clearly labeled with a sticker, in order to identify that the product came from suspected defective lot number. At this time, plans to replace the closed medication system were on hold pending replacement product availability.

 

During the night shift of August 17, 2016, a serious safety event occurred on a patient on critical drips infusing through the pressure sensing disc tubing from a suspected defective lot. Kerr convened the workgroup the next morning and revised the original action plan. The revised action plan was to replace all pressure sensing disc tubing from the suspected defective lot with new product. Kerr communicated the revised plan to clinical nurses through the Nursing Director group (Evidence TL5a-4, Smart Pump Tubing Issues Evaluation Session Meeting Notes, Safety Scoop Event, Safety Alert Communication Email).

 

Despite these efforts, safety events related to the pressure sensing disc tubing and the closed medication system continued to occur. Kerr guided the workgroup to the next unplanned change. A decision to cease the use of the pressure sensing disc for all critical infusions. Clinical nurses and other nurse leaders were charged to communicate the change to their areas and to facilitate the transition.

 

Recognizing the criticality of keeping nurse leaders and clinical nurses up to date and aware of the functionality of the pumps and tubing options, Kerr instituted meetings between TCH senior leadership and CareFusion executives. In the meetings Kerr provided information about the CareFusion product issues, assured support, and advocated to host a CareFusion engineer on-site at TCH.  Kerr’s objective was for the clinical staff and vendor to work together to understand the pump’s functionality, and to determine the best tubing solution that would not compromise patient safety. On August 22, 2016, after a full day of collaborating, testing, and brainstorming, the CareFusion Product Issues Meeting Workgroup made the recommendation to discontinue the use of the pressure sensing disc tubing system wide, and to replace the current stock of closed medication systems. Kerr and the workgroup reviewed these recommendations to ensure safety and efficacy of drug administration.

 

On August 25, 2016, Kerr facilitated huddles with clinical nurses, educators, and nurse leaders to guide them through the following immediate changes:

  • All Closed Medication Systems in-stock at TCH will be replaced with an alternate, similar product
  • Pressure Sensing Disc tubing will be eliminated from TCH.  All continuous infusions will need to be infused through a straight line tubing with no pressure sensing disc.

 

In her continued efforts to support nurses through this unplanned change, Kerr followed-up the huddle with an email to the Nursing Directors and clinical nurses to assist them and their teams. In ten days, Kerr led TCH clinical nurses through unplanned change of equipment failure with the hospital’s IV tubing supply. Kerr guided clinical nurses and nurse leaders to identify issues with products and processes, develop safe and practical solutions, and communicate the changes with others in the organization.  After the final changes were implemented August 26, 2016, safety events related to the replacement products decreased significantly and no trends of equipment failure were identified. (Evidence TL5a-5, CareFusion Huddle Meeting Notes, Safety Alert Email and Attachments, CareFusion Tubing Update Attendance, CareFusion Tubing Issues Report). The discussions between Kerr, TCH senior leadership and CareFusion executives, actions initiated by the CareFusion Product Issues Meeting Workgroup, and the utilization of this IV tubing product at many healthcare organizations, led to a national alert.
