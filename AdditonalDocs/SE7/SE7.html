Example One: Onboarding and Orientation Program for Nurses Transferring within the Organization from the Inpatient Setting to Ambulatory Services 

 

Oluwakemi Orogbemi, MSN, RN Ed.S, Decentralized Education Coordinator for Ambulatory Services, identified an opportunity to streamline and improve the orientation program to improve nurses’ transition into Ambulatory Services from another specialty care area within Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH). To enhance the orientation program, Orogbemi used the knowledge gained from her previous years of experience as an educator responsible for onboarding new and experienced nurses, and her own orientation experience at TCH in February 2015.  

 

Orientation Program
The Ambulatory Services orientation program previously was a two-day class that was not mandatory, which consisted of content similar to that provided in hospital-wide orientation for all employees. Orogbemi recognized that this two-day orientation program contained no specific information about Ambulatory Services, various specialties, or similarities to and differences from work in the inpatient environment. Based on nurses’ evaluation of the orientation program and feedback from Ambulatory Services decentralized education coordinators, in April 2015 Orogbemi discussed the opportunities for improvement with her leader, Umber Dickerson, MPH, Assistant Director of Business Operation and Strategic Planning in Ambulatory Services, and Dianne Scardino, FACHE, Vice President of Ambulatory Services.  

 

In May 2015, Orogbemi presented to the Ambulatory Services leadership team the need to enhance the program to effectively transition nurses into the Ambulatory Services environment. The Ambulatory Services leadership team supported Orogbemi and approved her to work in collaboration with the decentralized education coordinators to enhance the program by implementing changes to its structure and process. (Evidence SE7-1, Ambulatory Leadership Team Meeting Attendance Sheet and Agenda, May 2015)

SE7, Ambulatory Services Leadership Team

 

Process: Transition to Onboarding Program
In June 2015, the Ambulatory Services leadership team approved the new structure and process of the orientation program. The changes that were implemented to the Ambulatory Services orientation program in July 2015 included the following:

  • Implementation of a welcome email distributed to all nurses transitioning to Ambulatory Services.
  • Increased RN engagement in orientation through the use of outpatient-specific videos and content, with key personnel included in the orientation process, outlining specific roles and responsibilities
  • Revised communication, feedback and evaluation tools used to assess the orientation program, and completion of role-specific criterion checklists and pathways
  • Updated materials and resources provided in the orientation classes
  • Established leader representation in orientation classes
  • Inclusion of skills components and practical training

 

The reorganization of the orientation structure and process led to a shift from classroom-style orientation to a comprehensive program in which the classroom orientation is only one component of an employee’s onboarding experience. The enhanced Ambulatory Services Orientation and Onboarding Program has created a meaningful experience for nurses who have transitioned to Ambulatory Services.  Orogbemi, along with the decentralized education coordinators in Ambulatory Services:

  • Developed an onboarding and orientation cascade of responsibilities, which was distributed to Ambulatory leaders through a SharePoint site, providing specific details of the new employee onboarding process, roles and cascading responsibilities (Evidence SE7-2, Cascading List of Responsibilities) 
  • Established packets that included a “welcome new staff” document, clinical nurse job description, orientation pathway, seek and find template, objectives, contact information for decentralized education coordinators , guidelines and an Ambulatory Services directory
  • Instituted an orientation schedule based on role and department
  • Distributed an orientation evaluation form to all nurses to obtain feedback
  • Updated and revamped orientation presentations and materials to stay relevant through growth and change (Evidence SE7-3, Orientation Packet, Program Evaluation Form)

 

Evaluation of the Orientation Program
From July 2015 to August 2016, 27 nurses transferred to Ambulatory Services and successfully completed the orientation program. Nurses complete a confidential evaluation after each orientation program, and positive feedback has been received about their effective transition into practice and the Ambulatory Services environment. The 22 nurses who successfully completed the orientation program, are still working in Ambulatory Services.

 

In June of 2017, a survey was created to measure the success of the onboarding and orientation program for Registered Nurses transferring from another specialty care area within Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH).  An email with an electronic link to a survey was forwarded to the Registered Nurses that transferred from another specialty care area within TCH from July 2015 to August 2016. There were 22 recipients that were sent the email with 12 respondents, yielding a response rate of 55%. (Evidence SE7c-a, Ambulatory Evaluation Survey Results)

 

 

On July 3, 2017, the results were reviewed by Denise Tanner-Brown RN, BSN, MHA, CPON, Clinical Director of Nursing, Caner and Hematology Center, Oluwakemi Orogbemi, MSN, RN Ed.S, Decentralized Education Coordinator for Ambulatory Services, and Umber Dickerson, MPH, Assistant Director of Business Operation and Strategic Planning in Ambulatory Services.  Based on the survey results, Orogbemi in collaboration with Tanner-Brown and Dickerson, developed a proposed action plan with specific focus on the following three elements:

  • Overall outpatient orientation
  • Readiness to function in new role
  • Preceptor support

 

On August 4, 2017, at the Ambulatory Leadership Workshop, Orogbemi presented the results to the Ambulatory Services leaders to evaluate and discuss the responses of the survey, and recommendations for the proposed action plan.

 

The study results revealed that overall, the majority of respondents 64% (n=7) were ready to function in their new role post orientation, there were 27% (n=3) that felt neutral, and 9% (n=1) strongly disagreed. There was agreement among the Ambulatory Services leaders to modify the orientation curriculum specific to transfer employees and transition requirements, incorporate the organization-wide buddy program into outpatient orientation structure, and enhance preceptor/coach education and training. (Evidence SE7c-b, Ambulatory Leadership Workshop Agenda & Attendance Sheet, Orogbemi Presentation-Ambulatory Orientation Update, Follow-Up Email & Action Plan)

Example Two: Effective Transition to Practice of Advanced Practice Registered Nurses

 

Background
Texas Children’s Hospital (TCH) has been consistently increasing its number of advanced practice registered nurses (APRN). Following the development of the TCH Transformational Advanced Professional Practice Model, the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council, under the direction of Elizabeth Elliott, MSN, APRN, NNP-BC, Director of Advanced Practice Providers, created the TCH Transition to Practice (TTP) Program. The work group consisted of members of the council, who developed the program and associated tools. The TTP Program was subsequently approved by the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council and launched in 2013.

SE7, Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council

 

The Transformational Advanced Professional Practice Model and the TTP Program are based on Patricia Benner’s Novice to Expert model, which recognizes the importance of the progression of professional development based on a collaborative team-based approach.

 

The TTP Program supports all new graduates, experienced and returning to practice APRNs. The opportunities for role clarity, increasing skill level and development as a professional APRN were key in developing the program. Effective preceptorship and mentorship are also key program components.

 

For a new hire, the task of incorporating and developing new specialty knowledge and skills can be overwhelming. The overall aim of the Transition to Practice Orientation Pathway is to standardize the process of onboarding into an Acute Care advance practice provider role. Preceptor and orientee expectations are outlined to establish the optimal learning period for the new hire in order to lay a strong foundation for their advance practice provider scope of practice. (Evidence SE7-6, TCH Acute Care Advanced Practice Provider Transition to Practice Pathway)

 

Process: Transition to Practice Program
After a review of the current literature and discussions with other leaders across the organization, the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council determined that the TTP Program would consist of a phased approach to APRNs’ transition into the organization:

  • Phase I – Exploring the New Territory
  • Phase II – Building a Foundation
  • Phase III – Patient-Care Engagement

 

Phase I – Exploring the New Territory
During Phase I, the program supports APRNs by providing a structured shadowing experience with a trained APRN. The focus is on providing opportunities for networking with other APRNs and familiarizing the new APRNs with the departmental processes.  During this phase, the APRN has protected time away from direct patient management to allow for assimilation into the work groups. APRNs are encouraged to participate in department-specific learning activities and to begin networking with APRNs outside their specific area of practice. Phase I is anticipated to last for the first three weeks of practice

 

Phase II – Building a Foundation: Focus on Specialty Knowledge
Immediately following Phase I, Phase II of the TTP Program gives structure to educational content in the practice area. The program supports a didactic learning experience, allowing the APRN to spend protected time in an immersion opportunity related to specific patient diagnoses and management strategies that support best practice. This phase also begins the incorporation of the critical thinking skills needed to manage patients, with constant mentoring on patient management from the APRN’s preceptor and leaders. This phase supports the APRN to develop the skills and procedures needed to care for their specific patient population. The program provides a framework that supports case presentations comprising elements of competencies specific to APRN practice. These formal presentations are made to the work teams and to the section physician partner. Phase II is anticipated to last for three weeks of practice.

 

Phase III – Patient Care Engagement
Phase III covers the last six weeks of the TTP Program, during which the APRN is challenged to use the knowledge gained over the previous six weeks. This phase begins the hands-on direct patient management. The program is developed to support a gradual increase in the number of patients based on the APRN’s skill level. Frequent evaluations are provided by the preceptor to encourage independent practice. The APRN is given protected time to develop skills in case presentations, procedures and other skills needed for the care of the patient. 

 

Evaluation of the TTP Program
The responsibility for evaluating the Transition to Practice Program lies within the scope of the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council, as outlined in the council’s bylaws.

 

In June 2017, an email with an electronic link to a survey was forwarded to participants of the Transition to Practice (TTP) Program since 2014.  There were 25 recipients to the email with 11 respondents, yielding a response rate of 44%. One of the respondents did not participate in the program, leaving a total of 10 participants’ responses that were analyzed. The majority of respondents agreed that they successfully transitioned into their advanced practice role and received appropriate support from their preceptor (63%), leader (88%), physician (90%), and department (90%). (Evidence SE7d-a, Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council Charter, Transition to Practice Program Survey Results)

 

On June 27, 2017, the results were reviewed by members of the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council, Constance Lagrone, MSN, APRN, PNP-PC, Angela Gooden, MSN, APRN, PNP-PC/AC, and Karen Hizon, MS, PA-C and verified by Joseph Hagan, statistician and Elizabeth Elliott, PhD, APRN, NNP-BC, Director of Advanced Practice Providers and Chair of the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council. Elliot, Hizon, and Lagrone presented the results to the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council on July 28, 2017, to evaluate the responses and the current Transition to Practice Program including preceptor support.

 

The study results revealed that overall, the majority of respondents felt the program met their objectives and is a successful Transition to Practice Program, there were 25% (n=2) that felt neutral and 13% (n=1) strongly disagreed that there was adequate support from the preceptor. Although a limitation of the survey is the number of respondents (N=10), the data surrounding the preceptor is significant enough to cause review of the education, training, and selection of the preceptor. There was agreement among the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council members that preceptor selection is key to the success of the advanced practice provider. The Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council will move forward with developing preceptor guidelines, defining the role of the preceptor, and qualifications that will benefit the onboarding of the new advanced practice provider. There was unanimous agreement among the Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council Members, that the current objectives remain unchanged and reviewed after the next annual survey. The Advanced Practice Providers Practice Advisory Council voted to conduct an annual survey to gather inferential statistics to determine the probability that an observed difference between groups is dependable, or that this data finding might have happened by chance. (Evidence SE7d-b, APP Practice Advisory Council Attendance Sheet, Agenda and Meeting Minutes, Presentation)

Its completion is not a requirement for the successful completion of orientation, but a presentation date is agreed upon during the end of orientation meeting and scheduled during the monthly section meetings. (Evidence SE7-10, Truncus Arteriosus presentation- Andrea Sawyer-Gray, MSN, APRN, CPNP-AC) 

 

One of the best indicators of Sawyer-Gray’s successful transition to practice has been feedback from Mott and APRNs who she works with. The following are Gooden’s comments from Sawyer-Gray’s first annual performance review in November 2015:

 “I cannot say enough about Sawyer-Gray’s contribution to this team. She came as a new provider to a very busy and complex consult service and has done a wonderful job. Her success in this role is a testament to her hard work and mental toughness. I can't wait to see how she continues to grow in her role as an APRN and I am happy to have her on my team. Over the next year I expect to see her continue to grow clinically and to start adding other professional development activities.”
